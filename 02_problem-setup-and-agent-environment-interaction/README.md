# Chapter 1 — Problem Setup and Agent–Environment Interaction

## What this chapter locks in

This chapter fixes the event order and the primitive random variables of reinforcement learning.  
If this chapter is fuzzy, every later index and every later conditional expectation becomes harder than it needs to be.

By the end of the chapter, you should be able to say, without hesitation:

- what happens first and what happens second at time $t$,
- why the reward is written $R_{t+1}$ instead of $R_t$,
- what the difference is between an observation, a history, and a state summary,
- what a policy acts on,
- and what object reinforcement learning is actually trying to optimize.

Nothing in this chapter assumes the Markov property yet.

---

## 1. The interaction loop

Time is discrete:

$$
t \in \{0,1,2,\ldots\}.
$$

At each time $t$, the interaction has a definite order.

### The order of events

1. The environment makes information available for decision making at time $t$.
2. The agent chooses an action $A_t$.
3. The environment reacts to that action.
4. The agent observes the immediate consequence of that reaction, including reward $R_{t+1}$ and whatever information becomes available for the next step.

That order is not bookkeeping trivia. It is the causal structure of the problem.

### Why the reward index is shifted

The reward belongs to the consequence of action $A_t$.  
It is not known before the action is taken.  
So the reward generated by the action at time $t$ is written

$$
R_{t+1},
$$

not $R_t$.

This is why the return recursion later takes the form

$$
G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma G_{t+1}.
$$

The index shift does two jobs at once:

- it tells you **which action caused the reward**,
- and it tells you **when that reward becomes available**.

If you erase that shift, Bellman recursions become much easier to misread.

---

## 2. Primitive random variables

At the most abstract level, the interaction involves these objects:

- $O_t$: the observation available at time $t$,
- $A_t$: the action chosen at time $t$,
- $R_{t+1}$: the reward observed after taking $A_t$.

At this stage, no state $S_t$ has been defined yet.

That is deliberate.

### What an observation is

An observation is what the environment reveals.

It may be rich enough to identify the situation exactly.  
It may also be incomplete, noisy, partial, delayed, or aliased.

So an observation is not automatically a Markov state.

---

## 3. Histories come before states

Before you define a state, you need to know what full information is even available in principle.

A standard history at time $t$ is

$$
H_t = (O_0, A_0, R_1, O_1, A_1, R_2, \ldots, O_t).
$$

The exact contents can vary slightly with notation conventions, but the important point is the same:

- a history records the interaction up to the current decision point,
- and a state representation will later be some function of that history.

So the logical order is:

1. define the interaction,
2. define the history,
3. define a state summary as a function of history,
4. then ask whether that state summary is Markov.

That order matters because otherwise “state” gets treated as magic instead of as a specific information summary.

---

## 4. Observation, history, and state are not the same object

These three objects answer different questions.

### Observation $O_t$

This is what is directly revealed now.

### History $H_t$

This is the accumulated interaction up to the current decision point.

### State $S_t$

This is a summary used for decision making. Later, it will usually be written as

$$
S_t = f(H_t)
$$

for some mapping $f$.

### What this distinction blocks

A learner often starts thinking:

- observation = state,
- state = everything that matters,
- therefore whatever I condition on is automatically Markov.

That is wrong.

A state summary is just a summary until the Markov property is checked.  
The Markov property is not granted by the name “state.”

---

## 5. What the environment is

At the current level of generality, the environment is the stochastic mechanism that maps past interaction and the current action to future observations and rewards.

Two consequences follow.

### First consequence

The environment is not yet restricted to the narrow finite-state MDP picture.

It may depend on the entire history.

### Second consequence

The Markov property has not yet been assumed.

So at this point you are **not** allowed to say that the future depends only on $S_t$ and $A_t$.  
That statement is a later modeling claim and must be justified.

---

## 6. What the agent is

The agent is the decision rule.

It takes some allowed information and chooses an action.

That allowed information could be:

- the current observation,
- the full history,
- a hand-designed summary,
- or a learned internal representation.

Reinforcement learning is not just prediction.  
Its aim is to choose or learn a decision rule whose long-run consequences are good.

---

## 7. Policies

A policy is the object being optimized.

### Deterministic policy

A deterministic policy maps an information summary $x$ to one action:

$$
\pi : x \mapsto a.
$$

### Stochastic policy

A stochastic policy assigns probabilities to actions:

$$
\pi(a \mid x) = P(A_t = a \mid X_t = x).
$$

Here $X_t$ is a placeholder for whatever information the policy is allowed to use.

### Why stochastic policies matter

A stochastic policy is not just a deterministic policy with noise sprinkled on top.

It matters because:

- exploration may require positive probability on more than one action,
- partial observation may leave genuine uncertainty after conditioning on available information,
- and policy-gradient methods optimize action distributions directly.

---

## 8. The optimization problem

At a plain-language level, the problem is:

> choose a policy whose long-run interaction with the environment is as good as possible.

That sentence is still informal because “as good as possible” has not yet been written mathematically.

Later chapters will make that precise through return $G_t$, value functions, and an objective such as $J(\pi)$.

For now, the key point is this:

- the environment produces outcomes stochastically,
- the policy influences which actions are taken,
- and the object being optimized is the policy.

---

## 9. Boundary conditions and what has **not** been assumed

This chapter intentionally leaves several assumptions out.

### No Markov assumption yet

We have **not** yet assumed that a state summary makes the future independent of the rest of the past.

### No finite-state assumption yet

We have **not** yet assumed a finite state space or a finite action space.

### No value function yet

We have **not** yet defined value functions, because value is always value under some policy or under optimal control.  
The decision problem comes first.

---

## 10. Common confusions blocked here

### Confusion 1: “Observation” and “state” mean the same thing

Not necessarily.

An observation is what is revealed.  
A state is what the agent conditions on.  
A Markov state is a state summary that makes the future conditionally independent of the rest of the past.

Those are different notions.

### Confusion 2: The reward should share the action’s time index

No.  
The action is chosen at time $t$.  
The reward caused by that action is observed after the environment reacts, so it is indexed $t+1$.

### Confusion 3: A policy must use only the current observation

False.  
A policy may use any allowed information summary.  
Restricting it to a state summary is a modeling or architectural choice.
